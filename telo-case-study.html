1 The problem

A metric dropped.
Now what?

Imagine this - you're an early-stage founder with no dedicated person for analytics. You open your dashboard. A metric is down. There's no signal on whether it's real, no context on where it's happening, no suggested next step. The data exists. The decision doesn't.

Analytics tools were built for analysts - people with time, training, and a team to delegate to. Early-stage founders have none of those. The gap isn't in the data. It's in the handoff between signal and action.

"The tools exist. The data exists. The decision doesn't."
2 Research

Validating
the problem.

I structured the research in three rounds, each with a different purpose: scan, confirm, deepen.

Hypothesis
Early-stage SaaS founders need support to interpret their metrics and reduce cognitive load during routine business health checks, so they can make more confident decisions and save time - without becoming analytics experts.
Assumptions going in
1

Founders juggle insights from many sources - creating cognitive overload that makes it hard to know what actually matters.

Validated
2

A major source of that load is interpretation uncertainty - is this drop meaningful or just noise?

Strongly validated
3

Founders look for external benchmarks to compensate for small sample sizes and lack of confidence in early data.

Not validated
4

Founders spend significant time analysing product behaviour - inefficiently and at a mental cost.

Partially validated
2.1 Community scan

Lightweight scan
of public communities.

To check whether the pain was real before investing in structured research, I observed Reddit, LinkedIn, and founder Slack groups - reading threads and posting on targeted topics across 6+ communities.

→Finding. The pain was real and consistent across communities. Enough signal to go deeper.

2.2 Targeted questions to founder communities

Targeted questions
to founder communities.

I wanted to confirm whether the problem was missing data or missing interpretation - and whether founders felt the gap acutely enough to change behaviour. I posted targeted questions directly to Reddit founder communities and captured the responses.

→Finding. The problem wasn't missing data - it was missing interpretation. Worth a deeper conversation.

3.1 Opportunity solution tree

Mapping the
solution space.

I built an opportunity solution tree to connect the research findings to potential solutions - defining what Telo needed to address without jumping to features too early.

→The outcome of the Opportunity Solution Tree
Opportunity 1
Signal clarity

Founders can't tell if a metric drop is real or noise - they need a verdict before they can act.

Opportunity 2
Contextual triage

Once something is flagged, the founder needs to know where it's happening - which segment, which account - without opening five tabs.

Opportunity 3
Suggested action

The gap between identifying a problem and knowing what to do about it. Closing that gap is where the most time is lost.

These three opportunities became the design brief - each one mapped directly to a feature in the final alert card.

4 The user

Who we're
designing for.

Sophie. CEO of a 10-person EdTech SaaS. Responsible for product and growth. No dedicated analytics team. Checks metrics every morning to stay ahead of client issues.

Her morning,
mapped.

Five steps. Five moments where she gets stuck. Each became a design constraint - addressed in the order they happen during her morning, not in the order that felt logical on a whiteboard.

5 Design

From constraint
to card.

Slack is where early-stage SaaS founders already spend their day - it's the first place they go when something feels off. Building natively in Slack meant meeting them in context, at the moment the decision needed to be made, without asking them to open another tool.

Designed inside Slack's Block Kit from the start - not as a limitation, but as the medium. The platform constraint shaped every decision: what to show upfront, what to hide, what order information arrives in.

Testing rounds.
What changed and why.

3 participants · 10-15 minutes each · Task: "You just got this alert at 8:45am. Standup in 15 minutes. Walk me through your thoughts and actions."

The confidence score meant nothing on its own
Problem

"91%... of what exactly? I don't know if that's good or bad."

- Founder, B2B SaaS

Principle
Ambiguity effect

People avoid acting on information they can't evaluate.

Before
After
What changed: "Confidence: 91%" → "Confidence: real signal (91%) · 847 data points."

This was the iteration that reframed the whole product - the problem wasn't showing a number, it was making the number mean something.

"Possible causes" felt like the tool was guessing
Problem

"So these are just possibilities? How do I know which one is actually it?"

- Founder, EdTech

Principle
Hick's Law

Equal-weight options delay decisions.

Before
After
What changed: Two equal-weight causes → one marked likely, one flagged. Decision made before she finishes reading.

I resisted ranking the causes longer than I should have - it felt like overclaiming. Testing made clear equal weight read as uncertainty, not humility.

The CTA didn't say where it was going
Problem

"Investigate Durham - okay but what does that open?"

- Founder, B2B SaaS

Principle
Visibility of system status

Users need to know what happens before they commit.

Before
After
What changed: One word added. The hesitation disappeared in retesting.

The smallest change, the clearest result. Microcopy isn't decoration - it's the last design decision before the user acts.

No way to tell the system it was wrong
Problem

"What if the ambassador thing is a red herring? There's no way to tell Telo that."

- Founder, PropTech

Principle
User control + feedback

Systems that respond to correction feel collaborative, not authoritative.

Before
After
What changed: "Was this helpful?" + feedback buttons added. Came up unprompted in all 3 sessions.

Three participants asked for it independently. When that happens, you stop designing and start listening.

8 Reflection
8.1 Learnings

What I'd do
differently.

The hardest design problem wasn't the alert card. It was deciding what not to show. Every round of iteration pulled toward adding more information. The discipline was removing it.

1

The platform constraint became the design decision. Slack Block Kit's block limit forced progressive disclosure - not as a UX choice, but as the only viable structure. I'd map platform constraints before ideation next time.

2

Users need a verdict, not a value. Don't show a number until you've told the user what to conclude from it. Language is part of the design, not a label added at the end.

3

Research shaped the problem, but testing revealed the product. I came in with a hypothesis about information overload. Testing showed the real issue was information order. The research got me close - iteration got me there.

8.2 What V1 would add

What v1 would
add.

The MVP owns the alert-to-action loop for a single account. V1 extends the intelligence layer - but needs more rounds of testing first.

01

Multi-account view - which accounts are affected across the whole customer base. Outlier or pattern, immediately visible.

02

Personalised alert thresholds - letting the founder define what "normal" looks like per metric and per client. The same 15% drop means different things for different accounts.

03

More usability testing - three participants is a start. The next round needs to test with founders managing three or more clients simultaneously, and validate whether the @mention flow holds up under real pressure.
