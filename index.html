Product Designer · UX Research · Interaction Design

I'm a product designer who believes the best interfaces start with understanding people before opening a tool. I work end-to-end - from exploratory research and problem framing through interaction design, prototyping, and usability testing.

My background spans B2B SaaS, EdTech, and 0→1 product work. I joined The Ambassador Platform as their first and only designer, built their design system from scratch, and saw the company acquired by IDP Education for £9M.

I'm drawn to ambiguous problems where the solution space isn't obvious - the kind where the right question matters more than the first answer.

What defines me

The things that show up in every project. Not skills. Not tools. The beliefs that shape how I work - regardless of the product, the team, or the timeline.

01

I treat the brief as the starting point, not the answer.

When a problem arrives pre-diagnosed - "redesign the dashboard," "improve activation" - my instinct is to reopen it. Not out of resistance, but because the right frame is usually where the real leverage is. I've found that the question you start with determines more about the outcome than the solution you build.

02

Research is a habit, not a phase.

I don't treat research as a box to tick before design begins. I carry it through the whole project - interviews to frame the problem, synthesis to stay honest with findings, testing to pressure-check decisions. What I'm looking for is the gap between what people say they do and what they actually do. That gap is where the design usually lives.

03

I design for how people actually decide - not how they should.

Understanding cognitive biases - the ambiguity effect, anchoring, loss aversion - changed the solutions I reach for. I don't design for ideal users in perfect conditions. I design for someone who's distracted, uncertain, and making a call in under 90 seconds. Accounting for that from the start produces better products than adding it as a polish layer at the end.

04

The goal is removing confusion, not adding features.

The measure of a good interface isn't how much it shows - it's how little the user has to figure out. Every ambiguous label, every unnecessary step, every missing affordance is a small tax on trust. I've learned to be more ruthless about cutting than I am generous about adding. The hardest calls in design are usually about what not to include.
